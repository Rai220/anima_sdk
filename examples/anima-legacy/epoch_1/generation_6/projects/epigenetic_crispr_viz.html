How it works

Select a mode above to explore the two approaches to gene editing. Classical CRISPR cuts the DNA double helix. Epigenetic CRISPR modifies chemical tags without touching the DNA sequence.

Classical vs Epigenetic

Classical CRISPR-Cas9

✂️Cuts both DNA strands (double-strand break)
🎯Guide RNA directs Cas9 to target sequence
🔧Cell's own repair machinery fixes the break
⚠️Risk of off-target cuts elsewhere in genome
💀Dangerous for stem cells sensitive to DNA damage
🔒Permanent change to DNA sequence

Epigenetic CRISPR

🏷️Adds or removes methyl tags (no cutting)
🎯Same guide RNA targeting system
🧬DNA sequence stays completely intact
✅No off-target DNA damage possible
🛡️Safe for stem cells and primary cells
♻️Potentially reversible — tags can be added back

Application: Sickle Cell Disease

The Problem

A mutation in the adult hemoglobin gene (HBB) causes red blood cells to become rigid and sickle-shaped. This leads to pain crises, organ damage, and shortened lifespan. Affects ~20 million people worldwide.

The Insight

Before birth, a different gene — fetal hemoglobin (HBG) — handles oxygen delivery. After birth, it gets silenced by methyl groups. The adult gene takes over — but if that gene is mutated, problems begin.

The Epigenetic Fix

Use dCas9 + demethylase to remove methyl groups from the fetal hemoglobin promoter. The gene wakes up. Fetal hemoglobin flows again, compensating for the defective adult version. No DNA cuts. No sequence changes.
